Backpacking at Cottonwood Lakes

September 14, 2018

With that planning commenced and before I knew it Katie and I were driving up a narrow road with 180 degree turns to the trailhead. We had two very different ideas for the trip. I wanted to go as far as possible to get away from the crowds and Katie wanted to get acclimated to killer elevation. My plan disintegrated within a mile and it was decided we would go to Muir Lake for the first night, initiating the more camp time less hike time protocol.

I’ll be honest the idea for this trip originated from a photo on Instagram. A photo of a young white husky, smiling amongst tall yellow grass with a dark blue lake framing its head. This photo inspired two thoughts: 1) Memories of hiking with my dog when she was younger and 2) the desire to go where this husky had been. So I wasn’t left with much of a choice. I needed to visit Cottonwood Lakes before the snow fell, less I disappoint a very cheery and inspirational Husky.

Our second night was spent at a lake below the pass and above the tree-line amongst the marmots. We followed their example and perched ourselves atop the rocks to watch the minute changes in scenery, the deep blue of sky slowly transitioned into a pink that filled the lake waters reflecting the sky and mountain peeks. The breeze would come through stirring the lake water into movement, briefly distorting the mountain and sky.

The following day we hiked through New Army Pass accompanied by the familiar feeling of walking up endless switchbacks while staying stationary. As we inched higher we passed through spires of rocks towering over the trail, a small reminder of the San Andres fault running through California. To the left and to the right the mountain peaks spread out in a semi circle, the reddish white rock cutting sharply into the empty air.

At the top we snacked on salami, flat bread, and nuts before turning away from Cottonwood Lakes and following the gently sloping path down towards a grass filled plateau. The path wound through the grass where it divided, one path continued downwards, and the other continued up towards Mt. Langley (for those peak baggers out there). Naturally, after all the uphill we followed the downhill path heading towards Soldier Lake. The path continued along the plateau before dropping steeply into another series of switchbacks towards a dried up river bed.

Our walk continued throughout continually changing terrain, through empty rocks, flowing streams, towering pines until we stopped for the night at a small lake we thought would be pretty empty. Instead, we ended up surrounded by other groups. It was odd that the most remote place (farthest from the trailhead) was the most inhabited rather than our previous camps where we were mostly secluded.

The following day we headed back over the pass amidst intense winds. With every step the wind would rock us side to side pushing us off balance. By the time we reached Long Lake and the trees were bending over with the force we had come to the decision to head back a day early rather than camp amidst the high winds.

Mileage Breakdown

Day 1

Trailhead to Muir Lake — 5 miles

Day 2

Muir Lake to Long Lake — 2 miles

Day 3

Long Lake to Soldier Lake via New Army Pass — 6 miles

Day 4

Soldier Lake to Trailhead — 11 miles
